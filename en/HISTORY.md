# HISTORY (Summary)

> **This is a summary, not a full translation.** The Japanese document ([HISTORY.md](../HISTORY.md)) is the authoritative historical record and contains additional detail, citations, and caveats. This English page exists to give international readers the necessary context; for anything more than a general understanding, please refer to the Japanese original (machine translation is a reasonable substitute for most purposes).

## Purpose

This project aims not only to publish a license, but also to preserve and publish the historical background and primary sources behind it — including unfavorable records, not only favorable ones.

## Background

In the early 2000s, Japan's internet culture saw an explosion of original text-based culture, most notably ASCII art (AA). At the same time, this shared cultural property repeatedly came close to being enclosed by commercial interests.

## Basic understanding of authorship

Works such as "Gikoneko" are often described as having "spontaneously emerged from the community." This project does not adopt that framing. As best understood by this project, Gikoneko is the product of a specific chain of individual creators: a base face-only AA character created in 1998 by a fixed-handle poster known as "Neko" (now going by "Mamezou"), a body and tail added by another poster known as "Kottouya," and the name itself contributed by other posters. (This account draws in part on the Japanese Wikipedia article on Gikoneko, including content originally written by MOTOI Kenkichi himself in 2007 as a first-person account; see the Japanese HISTORY.md for full sourcing and caveats.)

The project's starting point is not "no one owns it, so no one can monopolize it," but rather "specific creators built this up over time, and a company later attempted to enclose it."

## 2002: The Takara incident

On March 12, 2002, the toy company Takara registered a trademark for "Gikoneko." MOTOI Kenkichi (Gikoneko) did not take action at the time.

## 2005: The "Nomaneko" controversy

In 2005, Avex / Netz Japan filed trademark applications related to "Nomaneko," a character derived from "Mona," a character that originated on the Japanese forum 2channel. This led to significant public controversy within the Japanese internet community. Having learned from the Takara incident, MOTOI Kenkichi took an active role this time, including direct correspondence with Netz Japan (preserved as a record; see [evidence/](../evidence/README.md)). The wordmark application was ultimately withdrawn, and the design application was withdrawn later as well.

## The purpose of this specification (AAAPS)

This specification is designed as a defensive structure against future attempts at monopolization, not merely as a nostalgic archive. It is intended to serve as supporting material when considering questions of prior use or public recognition, combining the permanence of GitHub's revision history with the Facebook community's activity record. However, how these materials are ultimately evaluated legally is left to experts and third parties; this specification does not preemptively make that determination.

This project does not claim rights over the original "Gikoneko" character itself; it limits its claims to what MOTOI Kenkichi (Gikoneko) has personally created and preserved (the name "Gikoneko," derivative characters, merchandise designs, etc.).

## About the name "AAAP"

"AAAP" is a name chosen in tribute to a now-dissolved voluntary association / NPO, the "ASCII Art Protection Association," which was active around the time of the Nomaneko controversy. "#AAAP" as used by this project does not refer to any current legal entity; it is an unofficial community hashtag used in tribute to that dissolved organization.
